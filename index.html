What is Bipolar Disorder?

Bipolar disorder, formerly called manic depression, is a mental health condition that causes extreme mood swings that include emotional highs (mania or hypomania) and lows (depression).

When you become depressed, you may feel sad or hopeless and lose interest or pleasure in most activities. When your mood shifts to mania or hypomania (less extreme than mania), you may feel euphoric, full of energy or unusually irritable.

  • These mood swings can affect sleep, energy, activity, judgment, behavior and the ability to think clearly.
  • Episodes of mood swings may occur rarely or multiple times a year.
Types of Bipolar Disorder

There are several types of bipolar and related disorders. They may include mania or hypomania and depression. Symptoms can cause unpredictable changes in mood and behavior, resulting in significant distress and difficulty in life.

The main types of bipolar disorder are:

Bipolar I Disorder: Manic episodes that last at least 7 days, or severe manic symptoms that need immediate hospital care. Bipolar II Disorder: Pattern of depressive episodes and hypomanic episodes, but not the full-blown manic episodes that are typical of Bipolar I Disorder.
Symptoms of Bipolar Disorder

Symptoms of bipolar disorder can vary. A person with bipolar disorder may have manic episodes, depressive episodes, or "mixed" episodes.

Common symptoms include:

  • Mania: Increased energy, decreased need for sleep, and loss of touch with reality
  • Depression: Low energy, low motivation, and loss of interest in daily activities
Causes and Risk Factors

The exact cause of bipolar disorder isn't known, but several factors may be involved, such as:

Biological differences, genetics, and environmental factors.

Biological: Physical changes in the brains of people with bipolar disorder Genetics: Bipolar disorder is more common in people who have a first-degree relative with the condition
Diagnosis and Treatment

Diagnosis of bipolar disorder involves a combination of physical exams, mental health evaluations, and mood charting.

Treatment typically involves a combination of medications and psychotherapy:

  • Medications: Mood stabilizers, antipsychotics, antidepressants
  • Psychotherapy: Cognitive behavioral therapy, interpersonal therapy
Living with Bipolar Disorder

While bipolar disorder is a lifelong condition, you can manage your mood swings and other symptoms by following a treatment plan.

In most cases, bipolar disorder is treated with medications and psychological counseling (psychotherapy).

Stick to your treatment plan: Don't skip therapy sessions or medications Monitor your mood: Keep a log of your moods, sleep patterns, and life events
